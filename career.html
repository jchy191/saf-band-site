CAREER

Joining the ranks of the nation's musical elite, a career as a military musician encompasses being ambassadors of the nation in an organisation at the forefront of diplomacy. Expect opportunities to perform for the President, as well as other foreign dignitaries at Welcome Ceremonies and other State functions.

HEAR THEIR STORIES

SAF BAND
CONCERT MASTER / WOODWIND GROUP LEADER
Concert Master / Woodwind Group Leader
ME2 Ang Yi Xiang

ME2 Yi Xiang is the Woodwind Group Leader of the Singapore Armed Forces Band. He also actively leads a team to choreograph military display performances during overseas military tattoo as well as for the National Day Parade.

Q: What made you choose a career with the SAF? What is your story?

A: The main reason I signed on was simply to continue pursuing music. SAF Band is one of the few good choices to do it professionally in Singapore. I took my LTCL (a penultimate-level Licentiate diploma awarded by Trinity College London) about a year into my service and I did pretty well. I was fully reimbursed for it by the unit. It is nice to have an environment where you can improve on your skills as a career requirement.

Q: You mentioned the importance of tutorship in the development of your musicianship. The organisation provides tuition for its musicians. Would you say this is one of the perks of why you wanted to sign on?

A: Definitely. Other than having tuition, we also get to utilize the tuition fund to attend master classes by visiting professors and clinicians. This is very helpful as it may be quite costly. In the band, it’s a generous part of your career progression and training as a military musician.

Apart from that, the saxophone section here is very diverse – we all come from different genres of music from pop, to classical, to big band, you name it. The interaction with my colleagues expanded my ability to play different styles, building my versatility as a musician.

Q: The SAF Band is gradually getting popular in the overseas military tattoo community. What are your thoughts about being the leading choreographer for the tattoo?

A: Music is internationally recognized. The magic happens when the band is able to bridge language barriers and communicate music through our military display performances. The execution of the marching drills incorporates diversity, adding a layer of depth to further captivate the audience.

Through all these events and performances overseas, I feel that we’ve made a distinct position for the SAF and Singapore on the world map. This gives new meaning to my career and is only possible in a military context; working in any non-military organisation simply does not offer such experience.

BRASS GROUP LEADER
Brass Group Leader
ME2 Ronnie Quek

ME2 Ronnie is the Brass Group Leader of the Singapore Armed Forces Band. He served as a Drum Major with the Ceremonial Band for about 5 years. During which, he attained his FTCL (the highest level diploma that the Trinity College of London awards) and was the first Military Musician in the unit to do so.

Q: What do you like about your job; why did you decide to sign on?

A: Well, I wanted to pursue my dreams of becoming a performer. Being here meant that I can have a conducive environment to practice every day. The rehearsals and performances with the band offer plenty of chances for me to perform. It also helps tremendously that the organization provides tuition with professional musicians, providing constant self-upgrading opportunities.

Q: What was it like juggling between being a Drum Major and a Musician?

A: I don’t think it affected my playing. In fact, I got my FTCL while I was a drum major. I feel that as long as we remain focused and committed, nothing will affect our standard of playing. Juggling between both sure wasn’t an easy feat and this is an achievement that I am proud of. The certificate definitely gave me more confidence. The improvements I have made leading up to the exam also opened doors for me to perform with ensembles such as Windstars Ensemble, Singapore Lyric Opera, and Singapore Trombone Society.

Q: Coming so far, it seems that you have done it all. We also understand that you are the videographer for the unit. How did you get into videography?

A: It started off as a personal interest – I did not know anything at all. After speaking to my superior, I was sent for a videography course where I learnt some useful skills. Putting these skills to good practice as a videographer for the unit, I would say I’ve came a long way from where I’ve started. I currently film and edit all the videos for the unit.

CHIEF DRUM MAJOR
Chief Drum Major
ME2 Hafis Amron

ME2 Hafis is currently the Chief Drum Major of the Singapore Armed Forces Band.

Q: Why did you decide to sign on?

A: Of course, the love for music. I was in a military band in secondary school and SAF band felt like a natural choice for me to start a career.

Q. Describe the journey to becoming the Chief Drum Major.

A: I was not a Drum Major in secondary school, but I always had an interest to learn. I spoke to my superiors and they allowed me to train for the role. I was subsequently selected to attend the Drum Major Course with the Defence Force School of Music, Melbourne, Australia.

The course taught me valuable skills and opened my eyes to the endless possibilities that a Drum Major could be. Since then, I felt that our drum majors could be doing so much more. Being Chief Drum Major, I am in an influential position where I can help nurture the newer drum majors in the unit, working hand-in-hand to break the boundaries of what a drum major can do.

Q: Being on the international stage where you perform in front of thousands, is there any stress, especially with your complex mace work and techniques?

A: Of course, there is always stress. Every day that I perform is a day in which I feel butterflies in my stomach. The exhilaration of performing as a Drum Major isn’t something easy to come by, and I appreciate and shoulder this role with pride. I get an adrenaline rush from this – especially when I’m doing high mace throws and the crowd goes wild. There are no words to describe this; the validation makes all the blood, sweat and tears worth it.

Q: What do you think is the future of Drum Majors in Singapore?

A: I see a lot of potential. The Singapore Armed Forces Band works closely with the Ministry of Education for the yearly Drum Major Non-Commissioned Officer Camp. My team of drum majors and I work tirelessly to structure a comprehensive syllabus for the students during the camp. It is great to be able to educate them about military traditions and the roles of the drum major at such a young age.

The future holds many possibilities for us, the courses for Drum Majors both internally in the SAF and externally for schools are still new. People are still just finding out about Drum Majors and its rich traditions. I want the future of our Drum Majors to be one that is vibrant, alive, and skilled. Slowly but surely, we will get there someday.

CAREER OPPORTUNITIES

Musician

Every new musician will attend the Basic Military Musician Course to be equipped with the necessary skills to perform his duties. The organization coaches its musicians towards an all-rounded sense of musical excellence with practical tuitions, music theory classes and aural skills training. The coveted military musician badge is only awarded to a serviceman after a series of stringent assessments, symbolizing the entry into the ranks of the Singapore Armed Forces Band.

The SAF Band Individual Development Programme In an effort to further groom our military musicians, the SAF Band provides many self-upgrading opportunities. These include practical and theory tuition from the Singapore Symphony Orchestra as well as professors from local music institutions.

Furthermore, examination fees with recognized music boards are also fully sponsored. These boards include the Associated Board of the Royal Schools of Music (ABRSM) and Trinity College of Music.

Principal Musician

Musicians who exhibit exceptional leadership qualities may be offered the appointment of a Principal Musician. Principal Musicians are tasked to oversee the overall musical standard and parade readiness of their respective sections. A Principal Musician will also work closely with the Band Major to ensure that all training and administrative matters within the section run smoothly.

Drum Major

All eyes will be on the Drum Major as he leads the band on parade. Only musicians who display exemplary conduct, personal turnout and drills will be selected for the Drum Major Course. The Drum Major is responsible to train and ensure that his musicians carry out high standards of drill when they are on parade. A Drum Major also needs to work closely with the Band Major and Director of Music to coordinate administrative matters with regards to parade.

Apart from parades and ceremonies, further opportunities await on the international stage during overseas military music festivals, where the Drum Major will be able to showcase his mace techniques with precision and style.

Band Major

A Band Major holds the rank of ME3, and serves as deputy to the Director of Music. A Band Major is very knowledgeable and holds decades of experience under his belt. This vast experience is necessary to provide advice on parade proceedings, to ensure that our military customs and traditions are all kept intact.

The Band Major is also in-charge of discipline and all administrative matters within the band. On parade, the Band Major works closely with the other Sergeant Majors to coordinate parade rehearsals such that they may be conducted effectively.

Director of Music

A Director of Music is a commissioned officer of the Singapore Armed Forces and holds a minimum rank of ME4. They take on heavy responsibilities as commanders-in-charge of a band.

The Director of Music works with the band during rehearsal to provide musical direction, ensuring that his band delivers quality music.

Due to the demanding nature of their work, selection for a new Director of Music is only from its current pool of servicemen. Candidates will be subjected to a battery of auditions and interview. Only the best candidate will be awarded a full scholarship to attend the Bandmasters’ Course.

The Bandmasters’ Course is held overseas at renowned military music institutions such as the Royal Military School of Music, Kneller Hall, London and the Royal Marines School of Music, Portsmouth, United Kingdom.

Eligibility / Music Requirements

Who can be an SAF Military Musician?
No specific level of music education is required. However, most military musicians hold diplomas from recognized music institutions (e.g. NAFA, ABRSM, Trinity).The performance ability and expertise required are equivalent to those of any major professional musical organization.

BENEFITS

SAF BAND

In addition to a competitive salary and attractive gratuity, a career as an SAF military musician comes with exciting benefits which includes:

Medical leave - 14 days sick leave and 60 days hospitalization leave

Maternity leave - 16 weeks (for servicewoman)

Paternity leave - Married servicewoman may be granted full-pay unrecorded leave of three days each on the birth of the first three children

Annual leave - 14 (‘O’ level / 18 (Diploma) days

Subsidised medical and dental care

Subsidised rates for holiday bungalows (both local and overseas)

SAF Group Insurance Scheme with coverage of up to $300'000
